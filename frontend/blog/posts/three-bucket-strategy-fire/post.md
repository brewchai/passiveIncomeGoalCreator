# The 3 Buckets I Use to Fund Early Retirement (Warts and All)

Everyone wants the optimised answer — the perfect allocation, the spreadsheet that squeezes out every last basis point. I don't have that, and I'm not going to pretend I do.

What I have is **three buckets** that I *gravitated* toward because they match how I actually think about money and risk — not because a model told me to. Here's the whole machine: a **cash bucket that covers my first ~10 years**, a **big equities bucket that grows untouched**, and a **locked retirement bucket I can't touch for ~25 years.**

I'll keep everything here in **percentages, not dollars** — the ratios are the lesson, and my net worth is nobody's business. I'm an Indian who left a US tech job and [FIRE'd by keeping expenses low](/blog/what-is-LEAN-FIRE). This is the money engine behind that life. It is not perfect. I'll show you exactly where it isn't.

## Why Buckets Instead of Just the 4% Rule?

The standard FIRE answer is: pile everything into equities, withdraw 4% a year, done. The math works on average. But I don't retire on *average* — I retire once, on a specific set of years, and the *order* of those years can make or break me.

That's **sequence-of-returns risk**: if the market drops 30% in my first couple of years and I'm selling shares to eat, I lock in those losses permanently and the portfolio may never recover. It's the single biggest threat to an early retirement — I dug into it in [can you retire at 35 with $500k](/blog/can-i-retire-at-35-with-500k), and you can stress-test it yourself with the [Sequence Risk Calculator](/tools/sequence-risk/).

My buckets exist to answer one question: **what do I sell in a crash?** The answer is *nothing risky.* I spend from cash while equities recover. That's it. Everything below is just detail on that idea.

## The Three Buckets

| Bucket | ~Share | Role | Return (pre-inflation) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1 — CD ladder (cash)** | ~27% | Funds my **first ~10 years** of spending | 4–4.5% |
| **2 — Equities** | ~49% | The growth engine (roughly years 10–25) | ~7% |
| **3 — 401(k), locked** | ~24% | Long-term backstop, untouchable ~25 years | compounding |

Roughly a quarter, about half, and the last quarter locked away. Let me walk through each — and why I made some choices that a purist would argue with.

### Bucket 1 — The CD Ladder (my first 10 years)

This is the bucket that lets me sleep. It holds **about ten years of spending** — literally sized at 10× my annual expenses — as a **ladder of CDs** with staggered maturities, so a chunk matures and frees up cash every year.

Why CDs specifically? I get asked this a lot:
- **Not just a HYSA** — I keep one, but I want the higher locked rate for money I won't need for years.
- **Not treasuries** — the rates were simply lower when I built this.
- **Not bonds** — the added complexity wasn't buying me enough extra yield to bother.
- **CDs because of the shorter maturities** — I don't want to lock myself out of *higher* interest later. A ladder keeps me flexible.

At 4–4.5% it's not exciting money. That's the point. Its job isn't to grow — it's to exist, guaranteed, so I never have to sell an equity share in a bad year. I manage the whole ladder manually, which is less work than it sounds.

### Bucket 2 — Equities (the growth engine)

About half the portfolio sits in equities, assuming a **~7% pre-inflation** long-run return. This is what actually funds the *back half* of retirement and outruns inflation over decades.

I don't just let it drift. Even now, I **trim overpriced positions very minimally to rebalance** — small, occasional trims, not market-timing. And here's a quiet rule I follow: **if I don't see a good opportunity in equities, I top up the CD bucket instead.** Cash isn't a failure to invest; it's a position.

### Bucket 3 — The Locked Retirement Bucket (and an honest confession)

The last quarter is in a **401(k)** — locked until 59½, roughly 25 years away. It's the long-term backstop I mentally write off until then.

Here's the imperfect part, and I'm not going to dress it up: **it's a 401(k) only, mostly for the employer match.** After that match, I didn't have enough left over to fund *both* a taxable equities portfolio *and* a Roth — so I picked the taxable equities portfolio, because I wanted money I could actually *reach* before 60. A textbook optimiser would probably yell at me about tax-advantaged space. They might be right. But an early retirement you can't fund until you're 59½ isn't early retirement.

## How the Buckets Actually Flow

The buckets aren't static jars — they feed each other:
- In **good years**, I trim a little from overpriced equities and let the cash ladder refill.
- If **equities look expensive and I see nothing worth buying**, cash builds up.
- In a **crash**, I stop selling equities entirely and spend from the CD ladder while the market does its thing.

And here's the reassurance that makes the cash bucket sane: **corrections rarely last 5–10 years.** In my experience the market recovers well inside my cash runway — so I can even *add* to equities after a correction, then rebuild the ladder once prices are healthy again. The 10-year cushion isn't there because I think a decade-long depression is likely; it's there so I never have to *find out* how long one lasts while I'm the one holding the bag.

## The Withdrawal Rate Reality (there is no straight line)

On paper, this structure comfortably supports a **3% withdrawal rate.** In real life?
- **Last year I lived on about 1.9%.**
- **This year I'll draw closer to 3.5%.**

Because life doesn't move in a straight line, and you cannot implement any plan perfectly. The reason this year jumps is real and heavy: a big chunk of it is funding my mother's cancer treatment out of pocket — I wrote about [what that actually cost, and what it did to my numbers](/blog/cost-of-cancer-without-insurance). A one-year spike from under 2% to 3.5% didn't break anything, precisely *because* the buckets were built for a bad year I couldn't predict. That's the whole case for this approach in one real example.

## The Honest Criticism: Isn't 27% in 4% CDs a Drag?

Yes. On paper, holding a quarter of the portfolio in 4% CDs instead of ~7% equities is "wasted" growth, and a spreadsheet will tell you I'm leaving money on the table. Fair.

But that cash isn't only a safety cushion — it's **optionality**, and here's the honest backstory: **I originally saved it to buy real estate.** I still might. It sits in CDs because it's house money in waiting. And if I *don't* buy the house? Then it's **dry powder** — enough fuel to pour into equities during a correction. Either way it has a job.

Is it the mathematically optimal move? Probably not. But "optimal" assumes I know the future, and I don't. I'd rather hold flexible money and sleep than optimise myself into a corner. Might not be the smartest allocation on a spreadsheet. It's the one I can actually live with.

## The NRI Angle (why US, not Indian, instruments)

A reasonable question for a fellow Indian: why US CDs and equities instead of Indian FDs and mutual funds? A few reasons:
- **Tax:** as an NRI currently in the **RNOR** window, my US CDs and equities aren't taxed for me right now. That's a real, time-limited advantage.
- **Familiarity and trust:** I've invested in the US market for years — my research, my instincts, my whole mental model lives there.

The honest caveat: **this is not settled.** RNOR status is temporary, and I plan to sit down with an **NRI tax advisor within the next year** and likely adjust the structure. I'm telling you what I do today, not handing you a permanent tax plan. Get your own advice — this stuff is individual.

## What Could Actually Break This

I try to know how my plans fail. This one's biggest threats:
- **Crazy inflation** — sustained high inflation quietly eats the CD bucket alive; 4–4.5% is comforting until prices run at 6–7%.
- **A change of plans** — buy the house, move, a big life shift.
- **Wanting more** — if I get tired of a lean lifestyle and want bigger purchases, the whole low-withdrawal math changes.

Notice none of those are "the market crashes." That risk is the one I've actually engineered around. The threats that remain are mostly about *me* changing, not the market.

## Should You Copy This?

Honestly? Take the *principles*, not my percentages.

The principle that matters is: **hold enough safe money to never sell risk assets in a downturn, and don't overcomplicate the rest.** Whether that cushion is 5 years or 10, CDs or a bond ladder, a quarter of the portfolio or a third — that's yours to figure out based on your spending, your nerves, and your timeline. Run your own number in the [FIRE Number Calculator](/tools/fire-number/) and pressure-test it in the [Sequence Risk Calculator](/tools/sequence-risk/).

I gravitated to three buckets because they fit how I think. They're not optimised. They've got a confession or two baked in. But they let me spend a market crash reading on a beach in Da Nang instead of refreshing my brokerage app — and for me, that was always the actual goal.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the bucket strategy for retirement?
It's splitting your portfolio into separate "buckets" by when you'll spend the money: a cash/short-term bucket for near-term spending, a growth (equities) bucket for the medium term, and long-term/locked accounts for later. The point is to spend from cash during market downturns instead of selling investments at a loss.

### How many years of cash should the first bucket hold?
There's no single right answer — anywhere from 2 to 10+ years is used. I hold about 10 years (sized at 10× annual spending) in a CD ladder, mainly because it lets me ignore market crashes entirely. A shorter cushion means less cash drag but more nerve required.

### Why use CDs instead of bonds for the cash bucket?
For me: CDs offered a better rate than treasuries at the time, avoided the complexity of a bond portfolio for little extra yield, and their shorter maturities meant I wasn't locked out of higher interest rates later. A CD ladder also frees up predictable cash each year.

### Does holding that much cash hurt your returns?
Yes, on paper — cash earning ~4% underperforms equities' ~7% over time. I accept that drag as the price of never being forced to sell equities in a crash, and because that cash doubles as optionality (a potential home purchase, or dry powder to buy the dip).

### What withdrawal rate does this support?
The structure supports around 3%. In practice it varies a lot year to year — I spent under 2% one year and closer to 3.5% another. Real spending is lumpy; a rigid straight-line withdrawal rate isn't how life actually works.
